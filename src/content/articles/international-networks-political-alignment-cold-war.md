  When do the international networks binding states together actually reshape their political orientation, and when do such ties prove politically inert? This study constructs a multiplex network of 149 states during the Cold War (1945-1991), combining a security layer derived from military alliance treaties (ATOP) and arms transfers (SIPRI) with an exchange layer based on bilateral trade dependence (COW Trade 4.0). Using United Nations General Assembly ideal points as a continuous measure of political alignment, the analysis estimates a panel model with country and time fixed effects to identify the independent contribution of each network layer to shifts in state positioning. ## English Overview

This article asks a core question in international politics: under what conditions do cross-border ties actually move state preferences, rather than simply reflecting them? It integrates alliance treaties, arms transfer dependence, and trade dependence into a two-layer multiplex network, then links network exposure to changes in UNGA-based political alignment across the Cold War.

## Research Design

- Temporal scope: 1945-1991 (implemented in five-year windows, aligned through 1990-1994 panels)
- Coverage: up to 149 states per period
- Security layer: ATOP alliance commitments plus SIPRI arms transfer dependence
- Exchange layer: COW bilateral import dependence
- Outcome variable: dynamic UNGA ideal points (Bailey, Strezhnev, and Voeten)
- Estimation: panel model with country and time fixed effects

## Key Findings

1. Security network effects are not universal; they are strongest in institutionally anchored regions.
2. Economic dependence does not produce a robust global alignment effect once baseline positioning is controlled.
3. Mean reversion in alignment is a dominant dynamic in many regions.
4. Regional heterogeneity suggests institutional context conditions whether network ties transmit political influence.

## Institutional Anchoring Hypothesis

The paper proposes that networks transmit political influence more effectively when they are embedded in formal multilateral institutions that provide regular coordination, higher defection costs, and elite socialization channels.
